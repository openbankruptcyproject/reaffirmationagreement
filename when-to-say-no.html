When to Refuse a Reaffirmation Agreement

The default in Chapter 7 bankruptcy is that your debts are discharged. Reaffirmation is the exception -- a voluntary choice to keep a debt alive. In most cases, the safer and smarter choice is to decline. Here is how to evaluate the decision.

Say No When...

The debt is unsecured. Credit cards, medical bills, personal loans -- there is no collateral to protect. Reaffirming unsecured debt defeats the purpose of filing bankruptcy. See our credit card reaffirmation page.
You owe more than the property is worth. If your car loan balance is $15,000 but the car is worth $8,000, reaffirmation locks you into paying the full $15,000. Alternatives like redemption (Section 722) let you pay only the car's current value.
You are uncertain about future payments. If there is any doubt about your ability to make payments for the remaining loan term, do not reaffirm. Defaulting on a reaffirmed debt is worse than not reaffirming at all -- you lose the property AND face a deficiency judgment with no bankruptcy protection for 8 years.
The property is replaceable. If the item securing the debt is not essential -- or if you can buy a comparable replacement for less than the remaining loan balance -- surrender may be the better option.
Your attorney advises against it. If your attorney is reluctant to sign the Section 524(c) certification, listen carefully. They have a professional obligation to certify that the agreement does not impose undue hardship. If they will not sign, they are protecting you.
The math shows undue hardship. If your monthly expenses (including the reaffirmed payment) exceed your monthly income, the Bankruptcy Code creates a presumption of undue hardship. The court must hold a hearing and can refuse to approve the agreement.

The Only Time to Consider Saying Yes

Reaffirmation may make sense when ALL of the following are true:
  • The debt is secured by property you need (typically a vehicle)
  • The property is worth at least as much as the remaining balance
  • You can comfortably afford the payments going forward
  • You have considered all alternatives (redemption, ride-through, surrender)
  • Your attorney agrees it is appropriate and signs the certification

Even when all five conditions are met, reaffirmation is a risk. You are giving up the protection of your discharge for that one debt. Think of it as an insurance policy in reverse: you are voluntarily removing a safety net.

The Rescission Safety Valve

If you sign a reaffirmation agreement and have second thoughts, you have a right to rescind (cancel) the agreement. Under Section 524(c)(4), you can rescind at any time before discharge or within 60 days after the agreement is filed, whichever is later. You simply notify the creditor in writing. No reason is required.
