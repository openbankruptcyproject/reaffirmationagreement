Reaffirming Credit Card Debt: Almost Never a Good Idea

Bottom line: Credit card debt is unsecured. There is no collateral. The entire purpose of Chapter 7 bankruptcy is to eliminate debts like these. Reaffirming credit card debt means voluntarily keeping a debt you could have wiped out for free.

Occasionally, a credit card company will send you a reaffirmation agreement during your Chapter 7 case. You might receive it in the mail or through your attorney. In nearly every situation, the correct response is to do nothing -- let the debt be discharged.

Why Creditors Send Reaffirmation Agreements

Credit card companies send reaffirmation agreements because it benefits them, not you. If you sign, you agree to remain personally liable for the full balance. The credit card company recovers a debt that would otherwise be eliminated. They have nothing to lose by asking.

Why You Should Say No

  • No collateral to protect. Unlike a car loan where reaffirmation may be necessary to keep the vehicle, credit cards are unsecured. There is nothing for the creditor to repossess.
  • The debt would be discharged. Without reaffirmation, this debt disappears when your discharge is entered. Signing the agreement is literally paying for something you can get for free.
  • You are in financial distress. You filed bankruptcy because you could not pay your debts. Taking on the same debt again after filing makes the financial distress worse, not better.
  • No second chance for 8 years. If you reaffirm and later cannot pay, you cannot file Chapter 7 again for eight years under Section 727(a)(8).
  • Credit rebuilding does not require it. You can rebuild credit through secured credit cards, credit-builder loans, and responsible use of new credit. Keeping old debt alive is not a credit-building strategy.

The Only Scenario Where It Might Be Considered

The only rare scenario involves a credit card secured by a deposit or property (a secured credit card or a credit card with a purchase money security interest in specific items). Even then, the items are usually worth far less than the debt, and you may have exemptions that protect the property regardless.

What to Do If You Receive a Reaffirmation Agreement

  1. Do not sign it immediately. You are not required to respond.
  2. Talk to your attorney. If you have counsel, let them advise you. Most will recommend against signing.
  3. Remember: doing nothing is the right choice. The debt will be discharged when your case concludes.
