Mortgage Reaffirmation in Bankruptcy

Mortgage reaffirmation is one of the most consequential decisions in a Chapter 7 bankruptcy case - and one of the least necessary. Unlike car loans, where some lenders aggressively push for reaffirmation, most mortgage lenders do not require it. Understanding why is key to protecting yourself.

Is Mortgage Reaffirmation Required?

Short answer: No. There is no legal requirement to reaffirm your mortgage in Chapter 7. Most homeowners keep their home by continuing to make payments. The mortgage lien survives bankruptcy whether you reaffirm or not.

Unlike some auto lenders who threaten repossession without reaffirmation, mortgage lenders almost universally allow you to keep the property as long as you stay current. The lender's security interest (the mortgage lien) is not affected by the discharge.

What Happens Without Reaffirmation

  • You keep the house as long as you make payments
  • Your personal liability is discharged - if you later default, the lender can foreclose but cannot sue you for any deficiency balance
  • The lien survives - this protects the lender's interest in the property
  • Some lenders stop reporting to credit bureaus - your on-time payments may not appear on your credit report

Why Attorneys Usually Advise Against It

The risk is enormous. When you reaffirm a mortgage, you restore personal liability on what is typically the largest debt you have. If you later default, the lender can foreclose AND sue you for the deficiency - which on a mortgage can be tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars. You will not be eligible for another Chapter 7 discharge for eight years.

Many bankruptcy attorneys refuse to sign the attorney certification required by Section 524(c) for mortgage reaffirmation because the risk almost always outweighs the benefit.

When Mortgage Reaffirmation Might Make Sense

There are limited situations where reaffirmation could be appropriate:

  • You want payments reported to credit bureaus and your lender will not report without reaffirmation
  • You have significant equity and are confident you can make payments long-term
  • You are in a rising market and the property value substantially exceeds the mortgage balance
  • Your lender specifically requires it (rare for mortgages)

Even in these situations, the downside risk is severe. Discuss alternatives thoroughly with your attorney.

The Credit Reporting Issue

The main practical argument for mortgage reaffirmation is credit reporting. Some lenders stop reporting your payments after discharge, which means your consistent mortgage payments do not help rebuild your credit score. However:

  • Not all lenders stop reporting - many continue
  • The credit benefit does not justify restoring liability on a six-figure debt
  • You can rebuild credit through other means (secured credit cards, credit-builder loans)
