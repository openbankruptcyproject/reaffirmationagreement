Reaffirmation Agreements -- Should You Sign?

When you file Chapter 7 bankruptcy, most of your debts are wiped out through a discharge. But there is one way a debt can survive: if you voluntarily agree to keep paying it. That voluntary agreement is called a reaffirmation agreement, and it is one of the most consequential decisions you will make during your bankruptcy case.

Reaffirmation is governed by 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c), which sets strict requirements for when and how these agreements can be made. This guide explains what reaffirmation is, when it might make sense, and -- critically -- when it does not.

What Is a Reaffirmation Agreement?

A reaffirmation agreement is a legally binding contract between you (the debtor) and a creditor. By signing it, you agree to remain personally liable on a specific debt even after your bankruptcy discharge. In practical terms, you are voluntarily giving up the protection that bankruptcy provides for that one debt.

Without reaffirmation, your personal liability on most pre-bankruptcy debts is eliminated by the discharge. The creditor cannot sue you, garnish your wages, or send the debt to collections. But if you reaffirm, the debt is treated as if you never filed bankruptcy at all. The creditor retains all collection rights, including the ability to sue for a deficiency balance if you later default.

Reaffirmation is almost always voluntary. No creditor can force you to reaffirm. No court can require it. The decision is yours alone, and it should be made carefully.

When Is Reaffirmation Used?

The most common reaffirmation scenarios involve secured debts -- debts where the creditor has a lien on specific property:

  • Car loans -- The most frequent reaffirmation situation. If you want to keep your vehicle after Chapter 7, the lender may require you to reaffirm the loan. Without reaffirmation, some lenders will repossess even if you are current on payments.
  • Mortgages -- Less common. In most districts, you can keep your home by continuing to make payments without reaffirming. But some lenders push for reaffirmation, and some debtors want it for credit-reporting purposes.
  • Other secured debts -- Furniture loans, electronics financed through retail stores, or any debt where the creditor holds collateral.

Reaffirmation of unsecured debts (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans) is rare and almost never advisable. The entire point of Chapter 7 is to eliminate those debts.

Section 524(c) Requirements

Congress recognized that reaffirmation is dangerous for debtors. A person filing bankruptcy is already in financial distress -- agreeing to keep a debt alive defeats much of the purpose of filing. To protect debtors, Section 524(c) imposes several strict requirements:

1. The agreement must be made before discharge

A reaffirmation agreement is only valid if it is entered into before the bankruptcy discharge is granted. Once the discharge order is entered, it is too late. The agreement must be filed with the court, and it must include specific disclosures about the debt amount, interest rate, and payment schedule.

2. The debtor must receive required disclosures

The agreement must be accompanied by a clear, conspicuous statement that explains:

  • The amount of the debt being reaffirmed
  • That reaffirmation means you remain personally liable
  • That you can rescind the agreement within 60 days or before discharge, whichever is later
  • That reaffirmation is not required by the Bankruptcy Code, by any agreement, or by applicable law

3. Attorney certification or court approval

If you have an attorney, your lawyer must sign a declaration stating that:

  • The agreement represents a fully informed and voluntary decision
  • The agreement does not impose an undue hardship on you or your dependents
  • The attorney fully advised you of the legal effect and consequences of the agreement and any default

If you do not have an attorney, the court must hold a hearing and approve the agreement. The judge will determine whether the agreement creates an undue hardship and whether it is in your best interest.

4. Presumption of undue hardship

The Bankruptcy Code creates a presumption of undue hardship if your monthly expenses (including the reaffirmed debt payment) exceed your monthly income. When this presumption applies, the court must hold a hearing even if you have an attorney. The judge can refuse to approve the agreement.

5. Rescission period

You have the right to rescind (cancel) the agreement at any time before discharge is entered, or within 60 days after the agreement is filed with the court -- whichever date is later. No reason is required. You simply notify the creditor in writing.

The Risks of Reaffirmation

Key risk: When you reaffirm a debt, you give up the protection of your bankruptcy discharge for that debt. If you later default, the creditor can pursue you for the full balance -- including any deficiency after repossession or foreclosure. You will not be eligible for another Chapter 7 discharge for eight years.

The risks are substantial and often underestimated. Read our detailed risks of reaffirmation guide before making a decision. Key concerns include:

  • Deficiency liability. If you reaffirm your car loan and later can't pay, the lender can repossess the car and sue you for the difference between the sale price and the loan balance. Without reaffirmation, that deficiency would have been discharged.
  • Underwater assets. If you owe more than the property is worth, reaffirmation locks you into paying the full loan balance -- not just the property's value.
  • No second chance. The eight-year bar under Section 727(a)(8) means you cannot file another Chapter 7 to eliminate a reaffirmed debt for nearly a decade.
  • Creditor pressure. Lenders benefit enormously from reaffirmation. They may pressure you to sign. Some may misrepresent your obligations or suggest reaffirmation is mandatory. It is not.

Alternatives to Reaffirmation

Depending on the type of property and your jurisdiction, you may have options besides reaffirmation:

Option How It Works Best For
Reaffirmation Sign agreement, keep paying, keep property and personal liability When you can comfortably afford payments and want to rebuild credit
Redemption (Section 722) Pay the current fair market value of the property in a lump sum When you owe significantly more than the property is worth
Surrender Return the property, discharge the debt When the property is not worth keeping or you cannot afford payments
Ride-through Keep paying without signing anything (not available in all circuits) When your lender does not require reaffirmation and your circuit allows it

Each option has different implications for your property, your liability, and your credit. An experienced bankruptcy attorney can help you evaluate which approach makes sense for your situation.

Common Reaffirmation Scenarios

Car Loan Reaffirmation

The most common type. Many auto lenders require reaffirmation as a condition of keeping the vehicle. But alternatives exist, including redemption and -- in some circuits -- simply continuing to pay without reaffirming. Read the full car reaffirmation guide.

Mortgage Reaffirmation

Less common and often unnecessary. Most homeowners can keep their home by staying current on the mortgage without reaffirming. Reaffirming a mortgage carries enormous risk because it restores personal liability on the entire loan balance. Read the full mortgage reaffirmation guide.

What Happens If You Do Not Reaffirm?

If you do not reaffirm a secured debt, several things may happen:

  1. Your personal liability is discharged. The creditor can never sue you for the debt, even if you stop paying later.
  2. The lien survives. The creditor's security interest in the property remains. If you stop paying, they can still repossess the car or foreclose on the house -- they just cannot come after you for any remaining balance.
  3. Some lenders may repossess anyway. Certain auto lenders interpret the Bankruptcy Code as allowing them to repossess if you do not reaffirm, even if you are current. This varies by lender and jurisdiction.
  4. Credit reporting may be affected. Some lenders stop reporting payments to credit bureaus if you do not reaffirm. Your on-time payments may not help rebuild your credit score.

The tradeoff is clear: without reaffirmation, you lose the credit-reporting benefit and may risk repossession from aggressive lenders. With reaffirmation, you restore personal liability on a debt you were about to be free of.

Should You Sign?

General rule: Only reaffirm if (1) you can comfortably afford the payments, (2) the property is worth at least what you owe, (3) you understand you are giving up your discharge protection for that debt, and (4) you have considered all alternatives.

Many consumer bankruptcy attorneys are skeptical of reaffirmation. Some refuse to sign the attorney certification required by Section 524(c) because they believe the risks almost always outweigh the benefits. If your attorney is reluctant to sign, listen carefully to their reasoning.

The question is not "do I want to keep my car?" The question is: "am I willing to be personally liable for this debt for the next several years, knowing I cannot file bankruptcy again to eliminate it?"

If the answer is anything other than an unqualified yes, explore your alternatives.

Key Statute: 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c)

Section 524(c) of the Bankruptcy Code is the statutory framework for reaffirmation agreements. It specifies:

  • The agreement must be made before the granting of the discharge
  • The debtor must receive the required disclosures at or before the time of signing
  • The agreement must be filed with the court, accompanied by the required declarations
  • If the debtor is not represented by an attorney, the court must approve the agreement after a hearing
  • The debtor may rescind the agreement at any time prior to discharge or within 60 days after filing, whichever is later
  • The agreement must not impose an undue hardship on the debtor or a dependent of the debtor

The full text of Section 524 is available at law.cornell.edu.
